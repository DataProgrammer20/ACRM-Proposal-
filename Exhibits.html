History of Computing

Computers, at their root, are machines that manipulate information. This exhibit chronicles humanity’s relationship with that information, including our use of computers. Artifacts such as a lifesize replica of the Gutenberg Press and possibly the last authentic remaining example of the world’s first commercial computer testify to the scientific progress we have made. The spirit of this room is perhaps best represented by the two roughly 4000 year old clay tablets displayed along with our more modern version of the tablet - the iPad 1.

Birthing the Personal Computer

Learn about the pioneers and technology that sparked the personal computer revolution. The room is decorated to represent the proverbial “garage” in which visionaries like Steve Wozniak, and Steve Jobs laid the groundwork for this technology. This exhibit also includes information on the first microprocessor, semiconductor, Jonathan A. Titus, Henry Edward Roberts, and more.

Electricity and Communication

There is a lot of technological ground to cover between the discovery of electricity and the digital computer. Much of humanity’s early experiments with electricity involved communications. This exhibit explores the contributions of figures like Samuel Morse and Nikola Tesla, illustrating the connection between the Pony Express, telegraph, phonograph, and the telephone.

NASA’s Influence on Computing

Learn about how NASA and the Space Race introduced the desire to miniaturize electronics for the first time. John F Kennedy’s famous “We choose to go to the Moon” speech plays on video, portraying the national focus and determination that went into this endeavor. On display are the last surviving mainframe computer used by NASA for the Apollo 11 missions and numerous examples of the consumer electronics that followed.

Neural Computers

This exhibit compares human brains and neurons to neural computers. It delivers information on the human brain, quantum computing, the first neural computer, and more.

Enigma

World War II necessitated all sorts of technological innovation, but many aspects of the 1940s had not yet been computerized. One area where computers did play an important role is encryption. Perhaps the most infamous encryption device in history is Nazi Germany’s Enigma Machine, and cracking the Enigma code became a major mission for the Allies. This exhibit explores the importance of this machine and the effort of the British, Polish, and American code breakers who cracked it.

AI and Robotics

Artificial intelligence (AI) is already changing how our world works, but what exactly does it take to consider a machine actually “intelligent?” This exhibit examines that question and takes a look at what robots and AI can offer the modern world. Keeping you company are some sci-fi AI celebrities, such as Robby the Robot and C-3PO.
